Stock Redemption in a Small Business Corporation — Gain or Dividend?

In small, closely-held corporations, stock redemptions are not rare events. They often happen when:

  • An owner retires
  • A family member exits the business
  • The company restructures ownership

The key tax question is always the same:

Is this a capital transaction or just a disguised dividend?

1️⃣ What Makes a “Small Business Corporation” Different?

Small business corporations are typically:

  • Closely held (few shareholders)
  • Family-owned or founder-led
  • No public market for the stock

Because ownership and control overlap, the IRS assumes redemptions may simply be cash distributions in disguise.

2️⃣ Default Rule: Redemption = Dividend

Under IRC §302, a redemption is treated as a dividend unless the shareholder proves otherwise.

Transaction Tax Treatment
Cash dividend Ordinary income
Non-qualifying redemption Ordinary income (dividend)
Qualifying redemption Sale or exchange

3️⃣ §302 Qualification Tests (Overview)

A redemption is treated as a sale or exchange only if it meets one of the following tests:

  • Substantially disproportionate (§302(b)(2))
  • Complete termination of interest (§302(b)(3))
  • Not essentially equivalent to a dividend (§302(b)(1))

✔ Family attribution rules often make these tests harder to pass.

4️⃣ Numerical Example: Small Family Corporation

Item Before Redemption
Total shares outstanding 100
Founder ownership 60 shares
Child ownership 40 shares

The corporation redeems 20 shares from the founder for $200,000.

After Redemption

Item After Redemption
Total shares outstanding 80
Founder ownership 40 shares (50%)
Child ownership 40 shares (50%)

Result:
Founder still controls 50%. No meaningful reduction in control → Dividend treatment applies.

5️⃣ When Capital Gain Treatment Is Allowed

Now assume a different structure:

  • Founder redeems all 60 shares
  • Child becomes sole owner
  • §302(b)(3) complete termination applies
Item Amount
Redemption proceeds $600,000
Founder’s basis $400,000

Capital gain = $200,000 Taxed as a sale, not a dividend

6️⃣ Interaction with §303 (Death Context)

In small businesses, redemptions often occur after death.

When §303 applies:

  • Redemption proceeds pay estate taxes
  • Dividend treatment is avoided
  • Step-up in basis often eliminates gain

✔ §303 is especially important for illiquid small businesses.

7️⃣ Key Takeaways

  • Small business redemptions face higher scrutiny
  • Dividend treatment is the default
  • §302 tests determine capital gain eligibility
  • Family attribution often defeats qualification
  • §303 provides relief in death-related cases
