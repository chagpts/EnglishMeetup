Why the Korean Government Cares About the Dollar

Background

Recent news reported that the U.S. revised its proposal regarding South Korea’s $350 billion commitment. This raised concerns in South Korea, especially around dollar demand and exchange rates.

Many Korean companies earn revenue in U.S. dollars through exports and overseas business. Instead of converting those dollars into Korean won, companies often hold onto dollars for future investments or payments.

As more companies keep dollars rather than exchanging them, overall dollar demand increases, pushing the dollar higher and weakening the won.

Corporate Perspective

Year-End Financial Pressure

Toward the end of the year, many companies hold large amounts of dollar-denominated debt. If the dollar remains strong, the value of this debt looks larger in won terms.

Government & National Pension Fund (NPS)

The Korean government can also influence the currency market through large institutional investors such as the National Pension Service (NPS).

Because the NPS holds massive overseas assets, it faces significant currency risk. To manage this risk, it uses currency forward contracts.

Forward Contract: Simple Example

Below is a simplified example of how a forward contract works in practice:

Item Details
Current Exchange Rate 1 USD = 1,350 KRW
Forward Contract NPS agrees to sell USD and buy KRW in 3 months
Forward Rate 1 USD = 1,330 KRW (fixed)
Amount USD 1 billion
If USD rises to 1,400 NPS is protected from losses
If USD falls to 1,300 NPS gives up extra gains, but gains stability

By locking in a future exchange rate, the NPS reduces uncertainty. At the same time, the forward contract increases expected future dollar supply, helping ease pressure on the exchange rate.

Key Debate
