Why Transfer Pricing Matters in Global Business

What Is Transfer Pricing?

[Disclaimer: This example is simplified for educational discussion purposes.]

Transfer pricing refers to the prices charged for goods, services, or intellectual property transferred between related companies within the same multinational group.

Because these transactions cross borders, they directly affect taxable income in each country. That’s why governments pay close attention to transfer pricing practices.

The Arm’s Length Principle

To prevent profit shifting, tax authorities require companies to follow the arm’s length principle. This means internal prices should be similar to prices charged between independent companies.

Simple Transfer Pricing Example

The table below illustrates how transfer pricing can shift profits between countries — even when total group profit stays the same.

Item Explanation
Parent Company Located in Country A (high tax rate)
Subsidiary Located in Country B (low tax rate)
Transaction Subsidiary manufactures goods for the parent
Low Transfer Price More profit remains in Country A → higher tax burden
High Transfer Price More profit shifts to Country B → lower overall tax
Total Group Profit Unchanged — only the location of profit moves

✔ Transfer pricing does not create or destroy profit. It reallocates profit across borders, which affects tax revenue and regulation.

Why Governments Care

Aggressive transfer pricing can reduce a country’s tax base. As a result, governments enforce documentation rules, audits, and penalties to ensure fairness.
